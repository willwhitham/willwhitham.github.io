Two new open access articles out now!

Posted on Fri 17 June 2022 in articles

After months of photography and intensive animal vision modeling, two papers on the sensory ecology of primates are now available open access!

The gaze of a social monkey is perceptible to conspecifics and predators but not prey.

We modeled how well capuchin monkeys, their predators (e.g., eagles), and their prey (e.g., small birds and mammals) can read capuchin monkey eye gazes. We hypothesized that capuchin eye gaze has evolved to benefit the capuchin, and that:

  1. capuchins should be able to read the eye gaze of their conspecifics, to pick up on social cues
  2. predators should be able to read capuchin eye gaze, to know whether a predation attempt might be risky
  3. prey species should not be able to read capuchin eye gaze, so that capuchins can hunt more effectively

This pattern of results is exactly what we found, even for each of the nine different variants of color vision that capuchin monkeys typically have. Read more at Proceedings of the Royal Society B here :: https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/full/10.1098/rspb.2022.0194. One of our lovely capuchin images was featured on the cover of the issue!

Chimpanzee (Pan troglodytes) sclera are conspicuous in ecologically-relevant contexts.

In a similar design, we tested how effectively chimpanzees can see other chimpanzees' eyes. Because chimpanzee don't have a white of their eye like humans, researchers have speculated that chimpanzee eyes have evolved to conceal where they are looking and make social behaviors more uncommon/difficult. But when we made efforts to properly model how chimpanzees see the colors of chimpanzee eyes we found no such thing, and instead found that chimpanzees can very effectively read each other's gazes for social information just like we do in our own species.

Read more at Scientific Reports here :: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-13273-3
