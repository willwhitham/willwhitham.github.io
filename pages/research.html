Research

Primate illustrations from the public domain Handbook to the Primates (1894) by Henry Ogg Forbes (1851-1932). Images from wikimedia commons.

I am a comparative psychologist - someone who studies animal minds to understand how they are similar to and different from our own. I work most closely with apes and monkeys as part of ongoing projects on the evolution of primate eyes. Some current projects are described below.

Simulating animal perception

Digital cameras are incredible apparatus for studying how different observers see the world. By combining an empirical understanding of a camera's biases with an anatomical understanding of an animal species' eyes, we can simulate how different animals would see any photographable scene. I apply these techniques to photographs from across the primate order to understand the evolution of primate social cues, especially eyes and colors, as they would be seen by the primates themselves.

Comparative eyetracking

The simulations above can model what animals may perceive, but complementary behavioral research is needed to test how animals actually respond to the information they are presented with. Eyetrackers are especially good for this, as they provide an incredibly detailed look at the way an animal viewed a scene. I use eyetrackers with nonhuman primates in order to study whether the animals act on the perceptions that our perceptual models suggest that they have.

Game-ified comparative psychology

Attention, learning, memory, and perception are among the domains of cognitive psychology, and it is incumbent on comparative psychologists to find ways to port these research programs to the animals we study. Computerized cognitive-behavioral games are a key tool in comparative psychology, and they're inherently automated, challenging, rewarding, easily parameterized, and ported to humans and animals alike.
